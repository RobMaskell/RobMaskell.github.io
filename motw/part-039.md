# Escape From the Golden Dawn, Summoned Cryptids, Serious Befouling and the prospect of Veiled Dawn Paperwork

December 23th 1896 - Later that same evening

*Being a Faithful Account of the Adventures of Those Brave (and Occasionally Malodorous) Souls Who Stand Between London and Damnation*

The company found themselves, as so often seemed to be their lot, in a room filled with boxes and the accumulated detritus of a secret society's hasty departure. The great pile of sundry items had settled somewhat, minus one box that had been liberated in circumstances better left unexamined by polite society. It was Gabriel West, ever the pragmatist, who gave voice to what all were thinking — that remaining in the building whilst reinforcements descended upon them was an enterprise entirely lacking in appeal. Samuel Fitzwilliam, the ancient and monstrous Elizabethan scholar whose ethereal form drifted about the edges of the room, offered his agreement with characteristic brevity. George Banks, the Divine sent from on high to preserve a world that seemed increasingly determined to make that task difficult, concurred with considerably less verbosity than usual.

Kyle Nugent, that most corpulent and capable of men, communicated through the peculiar psychic relay that Samuel provided, enquiring as to where precisely he should bring the carriage. Samuel, with admirable restraint, did not remark upon Kyle's secondary enquiry regarding the availability of toilet paper, and instead relayed the practical answer: the front door. Kyle would simply have to make do without.

Their egress, however, was not to be so straightforward. As the company navigated their way back through the cleverly concealed bookcase door and made their way towards the main hallway, the unmistakable sound of boots upon the front steps reached their ears. More than one pair of boots. Several pairs, in fact.

Gabriel, ever alert to the shape of a bad situation, opened what one might describe as his metaphysical third eye, casting his perception wide across the immediate circumstances. His assessment was grim: danger lurked at both the front and rear of the building, with the greater threat massing at the front. Capture — or rather, capture followed swiftly by death — presented itself as the most probable outcome should they dally. He shared this intelligence with the group with commendable economy of words.

George, demonstrating the sort of impulsive decisiveness that one imagines served him well in whatever celestial engagements preceded his earthly posting, located a chair and applied it with considerable enthusiasm to the nearest window. The sash frame splintered obligingly, and George cleared the remaining glass with the practised efficiency of someone who had, one suspected, done this sort of thing before.

Gabriel did not wait to admire the craftsmanship. He was already moving. Samuel, displaying what might charitably be described as a momentary lapse in tactical awareness, chose this precise instant to relay a message directly into Gabriel's consciousness. The ghost's timing was, to put it generously, suboptimal. Gabriel, his stride broken at the crucial moment, went sailing through the window as intended — but landed with considerably less grace than planned, his knee and quite possibly his head making vigorous contact with the cobblestones below, leaving him momentarily dazed and horizontal. The chair leg, having accompanied him through the window of its own volition, clattered beside him.

Sean, observing both the open window and the advancing gentlemen with firearms who had now entered the front of the building, made a rather more considered exit. Seizing the great velvet curtain — floor to ceiling, he employed it as an impromptu rope and swung himself downward with what Sean himself might have called measured caution, and what others might have called something rather less flattering. He landed, somewhat inevitably, directly upon the prostrate Gabriel. Sean's dog, displaying either remarkable loyalty or a catastrophic failure of self-preservation instinct, followed immediately through the window and landed upon them both, proceeding to lick Sean's face with considerable enthusiasm as the world threatened to end around them.

Kyle, from his position in the carriage — which he had manoeuvred to the wrong side of the road in the manner of a man who considers traffic regulations somewhat advisory — witnessed a chair exit the building through a window, followed shortly thereafter by two men and a dog in various states of distress. He drew his magnum with a hand that was, it must be recorded, somewhat slippery from a gastrointestinal indisposition that had been troubling him for some time. He communicated his willingness to assist through the only means available, which was to shout it through the window.

Inside, George and Samuel faced the advancing goons — for that is what they were, whatever those goons' employers might have preferred them called — with the resources available. Samuel, drawing upon the considerable repertoire of a developing poltergeist surrounded by an abundance of loose objects, raised every piece of detritus in the room simultaneously. Papers found eyes, objects found hands, and the general effect was of a particularly aggressive snowstorm of miscellany that rendered their opponents temporarily blind and incapacitated. George, seizing his fraction of a second, touched his burning sword to the velvet curtain. It went up with a whoosh that would have done any arsonist proud.

George then made good his escape through the window.

The burning curtain, hanging theatrically from the window above, continued its work as the goons outside encountered a scene of considerable confusion: two men and a dog on the pavement, a carriage on the wrong side of the road, a ghost drifting through the wall, and at least one velvet curtain on fire. From within the building, emerging down the front steps and using his sword as an impromptu crutch — for the company had, it must be said, made rather a mess of him during their earlier visit — came Reginald.

Reginald was not, it became apparent, entirely surprised by what he found. He regarded the assembled company with the weary resignation of a man who had anticipated exactly this level of chaos.

What followed was, by any reasonable measure, one of the more unusual diplomatic negotiations in the history of Victorian London. Sean, with considerable aplomb, produced the Holy Hand Grenade of Antioch from his pocket — that terrible and magnificent spherical device, stamped with a cross, which the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn apparently recognized on sight. The revelation that they had encountered this particular artifact before told the company rather more about the nature of their adversaries than any dossier might have done.

Reginald's subordinates lowered their weapons. They did not, however, depart. A Mexican stand-off of some duration ensued, during which the two parties made their cautious way down the street toward a restaurant that the Order of the Golden Dawn also apparently owned, as one does. Gabriel, conducting his own quiet reconnaissance during the small talk and menu discussions that accompanied this peculiar procession, noted two things of significance: the most vulnerable among the enemy contingent was a young, skittish fellow who looked as though he would rather be anywhere else in the world; and one of the enemy number had slipped away entirely, subsequently observed — by the driver of the carriage — heading toward a police box. The Golden Dawn, it emerged, had called the police. Reginald, when challenged on this point, offered the response that he had "altered the agreement," and that one should pray he did not alter it further — a sentiment the company found simultaneously infuriating and oddly familiar.

The negotiation concluded with the tacit agreement to relocate to the restaurant, though it was at this point that George Banks made a decision that could most diplomatically be described as ambitious. Gripping Sean's arm — and, via Samuel's quill case, Samuel himself — George activated whatever divine mechanism allowed him to transport himself across London instantaneously. The plan was the British Library. The execution was rather less tidy.

Kyle, in the carriage, provided what proved to be the decisive distraction for Gabriel's escape. The nature of this distraction — a wet fart of considerable resonance, followed by what was later delicately described as "following through" — scattered the attention of every soul on the street for precisely long enough. Gabriel, seizing the moment, rolled beneath a passing carriage with remarkable athleticism, gripped its undercarriage, and departed the scene entirely. The journey that followed was not, by his own subsequent reflection, among his more dignified moments. Several streets of London cobblestones later, his backpack was torn from his back by a raised curb and he was deposited, not gently, into the street — landing in what could be described as "the kind of soft landing that you put on your roses." He collected his backpack, now requiring to be carried rather than worn, and made his way to a park near the Order of the Veiled Dawn to consider his next move.

George's orb, meanwhile, deposited him in the foyer of the British Library in front of a considerable number of startled civilians, whilst Sean found himself materialized in the office of the Veiled Dawn's Director, Clarkenwell — the Holy Hand Grenade and Samuel's quill case having declined the journey entirely and arrived instead with Kyle in the carriage. The quill case, and Samuel within it, were somewhat battered from their adventures but remained fundamentally intact.

At the British Library, George Banks demonstrated to the head of the institution — by the simple expedient of removing his spectacles and revealing eyes of burning orange — that he was precisely what he claimed to be, an Ange; of the Lord god Almighty. The librarian, a well-read man in an era when well-read men understood the significance of such things, became immediately and thoroughly cooperative. George was escorted to the vault below, where the guardian of that remarkable collection confirmed what they needed to know of the essence of evil: it would be contained within a phylactery of indeterminate appearance, cold to the touch, and accompanied by an aura of such concentrated wrongness as to induce physical sickness within ten feet. He added, with some emphasis, that one should under no circumstances open it.

Sean, meanwhile, clothed himself in a deceased police officer's uniform, received what intelligence Clarkenwell could offer — the Order of the Veiled Dawn was in its final hours, making a last stand to buy the hunters the time they needed — and made his way toward the British Library.

His route took him through a park where Gabriel sat attempting to look inconspicuous while two police constables advanced upon him with the intention of relocating him to a poorhouse. Gabriel, in a moment that he would later struggle to explain entirely coherently, allowed his mind to wander toward the folklore of the Louisiana bayou, and in doing so, accidentally summoned the Père Malfait — a nine-foot swamp creature of the Cajun tradition, covered in moss, possessed of glowing eyes and superhuman strength, and notably disinclined to distinguish between its intended target and anyone else in the vicinity.

Sean arrived to find Gabriel in immediate danger of being hugged to death by a Louisiana cryptid in a London park. He dropped to one knee, unslung his express rifle, and shot the creature in a manner that would later be described as exemplary. Gabriel, given this momentary advantage, whipped his blade across the creature and redirected its considerable mass toward one of the unfortunate constables, then collected his backpack and ran. Sean ran with him, in the correct direction after a brief moment of uncertainty, and the Père Malfait was left to terrorize the park in their absence, alive and thoroughly agitated.

The company reassembled in a reading room of the British Library where tea had been provided, biscuits had been comprehensively demolished by Kyle (now considerably fresher than he had been an hour previously), and the Holy Hand Grenade had been returned to Sean's keeping by Kyle, who expressed that he felt "considerably more comfortable" in this arrangement.

The case for Samuel's quill required some attention. The quill itself was unharmed.

The world, as yet, remained extant. Though only just.

# Questions for the Next Session:
* Will Kyle's gastrointestinal adventures prove to be an unexpected tactical asset once more, or will the company finally locate a Victorian pharmacist willing to help?
* The Père Malfait currently roams free in a London park — will it take up permanent residence, and if so, will Londoners simply assume it is a particularly aggressive garden feature?
* Sean now possesses the Holy Hand Grenade of Antioch, a police uniform, and a dog of uncertain survival status — which of these will prove most useful, and which most catastrophic?
* Gabriel has the real tablet, is covered in horse manure, has lost his backpack straps, and has accidentally summoned a Louisiana swamp monster — at what point does he consider that the day has not gone entirely to plan?
* George informed the head of the British Library that the angel Gabriel is "a lovely fellow, actually" — will this create an awkward introduction when the actual Gabriel West eventually arrives?
