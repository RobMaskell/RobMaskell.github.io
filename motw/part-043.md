# Able and Sons, 3 Grant Wharf, Isle of Dogs - Part 2

December 24th 1896 -  Just after dawn

*A continuation for the account of the extraordinary events at a riverside warehouse*

## The Warehouse of Wonders and Woe
When last we left our intrepid band of investigators, they found themselves within the confines of a Victorian warehouse of considerable age and dubious structural integrity — a labyrinthine arrangement of wooden racking, stacked some thirty to forty feet into the cavernous reaches of the building, groaning under the weight of innumerable crates, boxes, and at least one chest of assorted cutlery. The bear, it must be noted for posterity, had already made its egress, having exchanged something of a spiritual burden with one Kyle Nugget, a transaction that had left the portly expert considerably worse for wear, nursing wounds both visible and otherwise as he brought up the rear of the party's cautious advance.

Gabriel West, professional agent of The Foundation and possessor of an increasingly bewildering suite of nascent magical abilities, had positioned himself high upon the racking — that second or third tier of rickety Victorian engineering — where he had begun to feel a most unpleasant sensation emanating from somewhere further into the warehouse. Like some infernal game of hot and cold played out against the backdrop of catastrophic structural failure, Gabriel moved carefully among the boxes, guided by a growing nausea that intensified the higher and further he ventured. The conclusion was unavoidable: whatever they had come for was stored not upon the ground, as any sensible person might keep their cursed artefacts, but as far from the working populace as possible — up amongst the gods, in the uppermost reaches of the racking.

## The Unfortunate Business of the Orangutan
George Banks, Divine servant of celestial powers and wielder of a burning sword, stood below with the rest of the party and determined that the moment was appropriate to attempt the summoning of his faithful snowy owl companion, Hubert. George produced his blade and cut what one can only describe as a celestial slit in the fabric of reality itself, fully expecting the arrival of a reliable, white-feathered assistant. What emerged instead was a large and extraordinarily irate orangutan, which surveyed the assembled company with the particular fury of a creature that has been yanked without consent from whatever it was contentedly doing. George, to his considerable credit, addressed the creature diplomatically: "You want Hubert? Not sure, Samuel. Not sure."

The orangutan was disinterested in diplomatic overtures.

Kyle Nugget, demonstrating perhaps the most useful application of his magnum pistol thus far, discharged three rounds at the creature, striking it in the thigh and sending it scrambling — with surprising agility, as orangutans are wont to demonstrate — up the racking opposite Gabriel's position. The shot rang through the warehouse like a thunderclap, ensuring that whatever hope of discretion the party had entertained was now thoroughly extinguished.

Gabriel, following the intensifying sensation of supernatural malevolence, climbed to the upper reaches of the racking. It was here that matters became considerably more complicated, for upon turning to survey the orangutan's progress, he discovered that there were now two of them. The Pukwudgie — that most cunning and shape-shifting of supernatural nuisances — had seized upon the chaos to disguise itself in a most convincing manner. "Right, lads," Gabriel called down to his companions, "there's two of them now."

The distinction between the genuine orangutan and the disguised Pukwudgie was, to the trained eye, a relatively straightforward matter: one of them had a bullet wound in its leg.
Gabriel, beset by the supernatural ick emanating from nearby crates and operating at a considerable disadvantage among the precarious and slippery surfaces of the upper racking, drew his shotgun and made his stand. The exchange did not favour him. The Pukwudgie-orangutan's tremendous reach proved decisive, and Gabriel found himself departing the racking in the manner of a man who had not planned to descend quite so rapidly.

What followed was one of those moments of extraordinary collective heroism that occasionally emerge from even the most chaotic of enterprises. Samuel Fitzwilliam — that ancient Elizabethan scholar, ghostly poltergeist, and gentleman of considerable ingenuity — reached out with spectral influence and dragged a sofa from the lower shelving out into the aisle below. George, demonstrating a most celestial grace, bamf'd to Gabriel mid-fall and wrapped him in protective wings. Kyle, for his part, positioned himself beneath the descending pair and took just enough of their momentum to prevent serious injury. The three landed upon the sofa with a tremendous crash, the furniture's legs splaying outward in four directions simultaneously before collapsing entirely to the floor. Gabriel, George, and Kyle emerged unharmed. Samuel observed all this from a comfortable remove, entirely untroubled by the laws of gravity.

"Just how I planned," Kyle observed.

## Cutlery, Glass Ceilings, and the Demise of the Pukwudgie
With matters somewhat stabilised — insofar as anything could be described as stable in the presence of two angry primates and a supernatural artefact of malevolent power — Gabriel rose from the wreckage of the sofa, reached out his hand, and attempted something considerably beyond the ordinary scope of a Foundation operative. Black veins traced themselves along his arm. His eyes began to bleed. Through what appeared to be a combination of raw fury and desperately channelled magical ability, he managed to pin the Pukwudgie in place upon the racking — not entirely immobile, able still to wriggle and hurl boxes at the party below, but substantially restrained.

The genuine orangutan, observing its companion in distress, began descending towards the ground with murderous intent. Samuel, with considerable aplomb, proceeded to poltergeist the contents of what proved to be the warehouse's cutlery section — knives, forks, spoons, and assorted implements whiffing about the creature's head in a most distracting fashion, causing it to cling to the racking with one arm whilst wafting desperately at the swirling silverware with the other.

George, meanwhile, completed a most impressive piece of magical architecture: an invisible barrier sealing the lower portion of the aisle like a glass ceiling. The result, when the orangutan finally lost its grip whilst battling Samuel's culinary assault, was immediate and comprehensively unpleasant. The creature struck George's barrier at considerable velocity and was, by the laws of physics, enthusiastically redistributed across its surface in a manner that defied easy description and defied entirely the concept of a dignified exit.

Kyle, gazing upward through what was now an entirely opaque ceiling of orangutan, observed the Pukwudgie above — still pinned by Gabriel's spell — and discharged his magnum directly upward. The shot found its mark in the creature's hindquarters, sending it tumbling from the racking. In its descent, the Pukwudgie transformed into a large eagle and clawed Kyle across the face — one scar added to what was becoming an impressive collection — before attempting to flap its wounded way toward freedom.

Gabriel, lying on the ruined sofa, covered in blood and exhibiting bleeding eyes, watched this eagle rise. He reached within himself for whatever reserves of fury and magical talent remained, spent three considerable reservoirs of bottled emotion in a single concentrated burst, raised his shotgun, and fired. The eagle folded in the air like a stricken aircraft, trailing what one might romantically describe as smoke, and crashed to the floor. The Pukwudgie was no more.

George's barrier chose this precise moment to dissipate. The assembled party — George, Gabriel, and Kyle — were immediately drenched in the accumulated remains of the genuine orangutan. Samuel watched this from his incorporeal vantage point and noted the two perfectly clean footprints where he stood amidst the general coating of crimson and orange. "This is shockingly not my problem," he remarked.

## The Chest of Phylacteries and a Hasty Departure
With the Pukwudgie dispatched and the warehouse significantly redecorated, the party located the object of their endeavours. A large tea chest at the summit of the racking, marked in painted letters: Phylacteries (various). Lowered by means of Victorian block-and-tackle apparatus — Heath Robinson in execution but serviceable in result — the chest was opened to reveal nine compartments, of which four were occupied by large, ornate glass bottles sealed with corks, wax, string, and warding symbols of considerable intricacy.

The process of identification was empirical. Gabriel took up the first — a fingernails-down-the-blackboard sensation in the mind, vibrating, but not cold. Not that one. Kyle retrieved the next — scalding hot, dropped immediately. "Very, very hot. Do not recommend." The fourth produced the same unpleasant vibration as the first.

George reached for the third. What followed was, in his own words, "the most repulsive thing I have felt in my entire existence." Cold to the touch, and producing in an angelic being of divine origin a reaction of such visceral horror that he fell to one knee. "If I were a gambling man, I would say that this is the one."

Gabriel wrapped it carefully in straw, placed it in his satchel, and took the weight of its malevolence upon himself. The party then turned to leave — only to find, rounding the corner toward the exit, two gentlemen with loaded shotguns who had been somewhat alarmed by the preceding forty minutes of gunfire, crashing furniture, and supernatural bestiary.

Kyle attempted to persuade them that something terrible was coming. The gentlemen, surveying three individuals absolutely coated in what appeared to be blood, entrails, and other substances best left unidentified, were unconvinced. George, exercising a certain celestial creativity, summoned — and it must be noted, on this occasion successfully — Bigfoot into the warehouse on the far side of the gate. The two gentlemen promptly opened fire on Bigfoot, the party slipped past in the resulting confusion, and George, in a final act of conscientious damage limitation, sealed the building's entrance with a barring spell to contain the now extremely agitated cryptid within. He offered sincere apologies through the door as he ran.

Gabriel, taking the driver's seat of the carriage — the driver having achieved a state of helpless dry retching that precluded any professional function — cracked the whip as the first light of dawn spread across the East End. He paused only for a moment, turning to survey the approaching storm clouds building from the southeast with the expression of a man who has earned a genuinely dramatic moment. "There's a storm coming," he said, and hopped into the carriage.

## Clarissa, Camden, and a Celestial Kidnapping
After ablutions of questionable efficacy in the Thames, a change of clothes procured from a somewhat startled Saint Paul's Cathedral — Kyle's outfit being particularly notable, consisting of mismatched vestments and a workman's flat cap — and the acquisition of frankincense and myrrh from the nativity arrangement, the party arrived at a modest terraced house in Camden at approximately six in the morning on Christmas Eve.

George knocked. The occupant — Clarissa, former wife of Kyle Nugget, described with unusual warmth by Kyle as "a nice woman" — was understandably reluctant to receive visitors at such an hour. George, with commendable directness, admitted: "Kyle Nugget said you were a trusted person, and we should come to you in our hour of need."

The door cracked open. Clarissa's eyes found Kyle lurking at the back of the group. She opened the door fully. "Kyle," she said, "you said I'd never see you again."
"I am genuinely sorry that I accidentally sacrificed your brother," Kyle offered. "I knew someone would go. I didn't know it'd be him." He presented the myrrh. She accepted it. George's foot found the door frame before the door could close.

Clarissa was let in on the full gravity of the situation when George removed his spectacles and his wings emerged. She fainted. The myrrh was wafted under her nose. Kyle filled in certain historical gaps for George regarding the nature of his previous relationship with Clarissa, including the revelation that the brother had been an active participant in a ritual intended to resurrect the family dog, and that Kyle's momentary distraction had redirected the working to rather different ends.

"Did the dog come back?" George enquired. "No," said Kyle. "He's what they call super dead."

Gabriel, meanwhile, had excused himself to the small back yard with the phylactery, stabilising his wounds in the cold morning air and keeping the essence of evil at a reasonable distance from the civilians within.

Plans were forming. Samuel offered his services as an extraordinarily painful healer to any who required it. George prepared to heal himself. And then — with the particular crack that the party had come to associate with celestial intervention — two figures appeared, one on either side of George. They regarded the scene with the cold efficiency of those who have seen considerably worse and found it only mildly interesting. "George," they said, "you are coming with us."

And then they were gone.

George Banks, Divine, angelic being, and summoner of cryptids, was taken to account.

Thus ended one of the more eventful nights in the recent history of The Order of the Veiled Dawn. The phylactery was secured. The Pukwudgie was defeated. Bigfoot was briefly introduced to the Isle of Dogs. Two warehouse employees had an experience they would struggle to explain at any dinner party. And George, who had this evening summoned an orangutan, a Bigfoot, and indirectly liquefied an endangered primate, was apparently overdue for a conversation with management.

The storm from the southeast continued to build.

# Questions for the Next Session:
* Will George's celestial debrief be more or less uncomfortable than the time he accidentally created an orangutan ceiling, and is there a formal complaints procedure for the summoning of Bigfoot on Christmas Eve?
* Kyle managed to apologise to Clarissa for accidentally sacrificing her brother — will she forgive him, and more pressingly, will she allow a supernatural ritual to be conducted in her parlour before the mince pies are even out?
* Gabriel is now carrying a bottle of concentrated supernatural evil in a satchel whilst being actively unstable and bleeding from the eyes — what could possibly go wrong when Samuel eventually performs his "extraordinarily painful" healing?
* With Bigfoot now loose in a Victorian warehouse in the Isle of Dogs, how long before he migrates to Balham, discovers artisan coffee, and opens a technology consultancy?
* The driver of the carriage has now witnessed orangutans, gunfire, Bigfoot, an angel's wings, and a woman fainting — is he still employed, and if so, does he qualify for a substantial hazard pay increase?
