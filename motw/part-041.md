# Heading to the Warehouse after the Essence of Evil

December 24th 1896 - Absurdly Early in the Morning

*Being a True and Faithful Record of some of the Events of the Twenty-Fourth of December*

The night had not been kind to our assembled company. The grand vaults of the British Museum's library, ordinarily a place of quiet scholarship and dusty reverence, had been reduced to a scene of considerable chaos. Smoke billowed from the lower levels, and the frantic passage of men carrying buckets of water in desperate chains painted a picture of Victorian industry at its most frantic. The demons had been faced, the vault had been — rather adventurously — set ablaze, and now the hunters found themselves in the uncomfortable position of survivors who had caused rather more damage than intended.

It was into this atmosphere that George Banks, the Divine made flesh, found himself alone in an office before the head librarian, a man of considerable displeasure seated behind a desk notable for the absence of the quill case that ought to have occupied its shelf. George had turned to seek the reassuring presence of Sean at his back, only to discover that the fire-loving Mundane had, in the way of fires everywhere, vanished entirely. The office was quiet save for the crackling of distant smoke and the head librarian's gathering indignation.

The librarian made his displeasure known with admirable precision. A deal had been struck, he insisted. The hunters were to deal with the things in the vault. Instead, they had apparently torched much of it. George, for his part, was magnificently unruffled. "You invited me over tea and biscuits," he observed with the air of a man stating an incontrovertible fact, "before you know it, we're fighting demons in a vault. Would you care to explain yourself?" The matter of the drugged biscuits was raised, and the head librarian protested that no drugging had occurred — merely a deal, honoured in the breach. George fixed him with the particular gaze of a being who has witnessed considerably more of human failing than most, and outlined his terms with brisk efficiency: lock the library down, gather all personnel, and test each of them with the word Christo — for demons, exposed to that invocation, would reveal themselves through involuntary reaction.

The librarian remained unconvinced of the gravity of the situation, and George's patience, always a finite resource at the best of times, reached its natural conclusion. What followed was, by any measure, one of the more remarkable scenes in the library's long history. The wings of an angel of the Divine unfolded to their full and considerable span, books were knocked from shelves with an absolute lack of apology, and the head librarian found himself seized by the scruff of the neck by a glowing, very cross celestial being. "Stop playing games you don't understand," George informed him at close range, in a manner more reminiscent of a Bermondsey criminal than a herald of heaven. "Release my friends, provide them with what they need." The librarian, to his lasting credit, agreed immediately and with great enthusiasm.

George swept from the office — pausing only to replace his spectacles with studied composure before slamming the door — and was reunited with Gabriel West and Kyle Nugget, both of whom were still recovering from the considerable pharmaceutical properties of the library's hospitality biscuits. Kyle, who had applied himself to the biscuit plate with characteristic dedication, was markedly groggier than Gabriel, who was approaching something resembling operational readiness. George provided a brisk summary of recent events: three demons encountered in the vault, one captured in an Egyptian clay jar with a well-fitting cork, one banished by holy light, and one — the most powerful of the trio — potentially at large. The library had been sealed on George's instruction. The Christo test had been administered. No reactions had been observed.

The situation, however, remained vexed on several fronts. Their equipment — every sword cane, hand grenade, quill case, tablet, and backpack — had been confiscated. Samuel Fitzwilliam, that most monstrous and ancient of Elizabethan scholars, was conspicuously absent from proceedings. And Sean, last seen departing in the direction of what the librarian's staff charitably described as the water closet, had not returned. George admitted with some concern that his celestial ability to locate Sean by orb had failed entirely — a troubling development that had not occurred before.

It was Samuel himself who resolved the mystery of his own whereabouts. Emerging cautiously from his quill case in a dark room some hundred metres distant, the ghostly scholar found himself surrounded by the entirety of the party's confiscated equipment — and also by a dead body, its hand wrapped around the raven-shaped dagger of considerable occult significance. Samuel, with the analytical detachment of centuries of scholarship, assessed this as a classic locked room mystery, though he prudently declined to touch the dagger himself, recognising the instrument for what it was and what it apparently did to those who handled it uninvited.

The reunification of hunters and equipment proceeded with gratifying speed once Gabriel produced, from somewhere about his person, a pink damage insurance form bearing the Foundation's address and contact details. The head librarian, his sails somewhat taken out of the wind by this bureaucratic efficiency, accepted the form and was persuaded — through a combination of paperwork and the lingering memory of angelic wings — to expect correspondence through proper channels. Kyle claimed the dagger. The hand grenade was entrusted to Kyle on the reasonable grounds that Sean had demonstrated a pattern of losing it. Gabriel took charge of Sean's express rifle alongside the real tablet. George, in a reflection of his colleagues' assessment of his impulse control, was allocated the fake tablet and his sword cane, and was quietly relieved to have the latter back.

Outside, on one of the gate posts, Hubert the Snowy Owl awaited them with the bearing of a creature who has been sent to deliver unwelcome news and intends to do so thoroughly. The message from Upstairs was unambiguous: George was to cease smiting until further notice. It was the considered view of celestial management that extended time on earth had compromised his ability to reliably distinguish good from evil — an assessment George received with the equanimity of a man who has just fought three demons before sunrise on Christmas Eve and is not in the mood for institutional criticism. His response, delivered for relay to the relevant authorities above, was not fit for polite print. Hubert, somewhat mollified by George's subsequent entreaty that he remain with the party through what was clearly approaching a climax of some significance, smoothed his feathers and elected to stay. He also, in a gesture of considerable thoughtfulness, deposited a bag of bacon into Kyle's hands. Kyle, still somewhat wan from the biscuit affair, consumed it with the focused gratitude of a man who has found his anchor in troubled waters.

The carriage — gaudy, golden, and still very much in service — awaited them. Gabriel, who had spent a portion of the previous evening's escapades travelling beneath a coach through the less salubrious aspects of Victorian street drainage, was encouraged by the driver to consider sitting up front for the journey. Gabriel addressed this suggestion with the full weight of professional displeasure, crossed a star from a small card documenting the driver's performance, and took his place inside. George, exercising what he described as entirely practical judgment, elected to ride on the footplate at the rear. The Isle of Dogs was their destination: Abel and Sons, Three Grand Wharf, the address from the packing boxes discovered at the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn's abandoned premises — the last known lead on the Essence of Evil, that final component required to complete the tablet and bring this long endeavour to its conclusion.

The journey afforded a brief interlude of recovery. Gabriel tended to his wounds with the methodical competence of a Foundation professional. George attempted a laying on of hands, healed one wound, and immediately acquired a troublesome back pain in its place — suffering this with the quiet dignity of a being rediscovering the many inconveniences of occupying a physical form. Kyle worked at the hand grenade with a cloth, attempting to remove bacon grease from it, with results that were partial at best.

They arrived at the warehouse as the darkness of a Victorian December predawn still held the Isle of Dogs in its grip — an industrial stretch of canals and brick, large enough to have swallowed whatever the Golden Dawn had abandoned from their mansion premises. The building was substantial: a warehouse of some hundreds of metres in length, its windows dark, Abel and Sons painted in white on its flank. Gabriel read the situation with professional care, and the agreed approach was to send Kyle and Samuel forward as a reconnaissance team, Samuel scanning ahead through the building's interior as only a centuries-old ghost may, while Gabriel sought an elevated position with Sean's rifle as overwatch, and George — declining to transport the tablet by angelic means given its documented aversion to such travel — committed himself to running.

Kyle set off toward the warehouse, hands in pockets, whistling with the studied casualness of a very large man attempting to look as though he were not casing a warehouse at five in the morning on Christmas Eve.

Sean, however, remained unaccounted for. Somewhere between the library and the Isle of Dogs, between the vault and the present, the fire-starting Mundane had slipped away — or been taken. George could not locate him. No black eyes had been observed among the library staff. Whether Sean had been seized by the demon that escaped, possessed, hidden, or simply wandered off in search of something combustible, remained entirely unknown.
The party stood poised on the threshold of what all present sensed was the final chapter of a very long and frequently explosive story.


# Questions for the Next Session:
* Sean was last seen heading toward the water closet — but given his well-documented relationship with fire and chaos, is it possible that he has simply found something in the warehouse to set alight and is now trapped inside his own handiwork?
* Kyle has successfully removed the grenade from Sean's care and placed it in his own — given his current condition of bacon-grease-covered fingers and residual biscuit-induced grogginess, should the party be more or less worried?
* George has been formally prohibited from smiting and has responded by telling Upstairs to go and do something anatomically inadvisable — will his next disciplinary review be conducted before or after the world ends, and does the committee meet on Christmas Day?
* The demon in the clay jar has a well-fitting cork lid that the party has been advised to secure more permanently — given the party's track record, what are the odds that this reminder has been entirely forgotten and the jar is currently rolling around loose in the carriage?
* Samuel has spent centuries in a library, and the head librarian has now personally invited him to return to gainful employment — if the world is saved, is it possible that the most eldritch member of the party ends up with the most respectable job?
