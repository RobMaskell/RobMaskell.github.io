# The Ex-wife, the Possessed Friend and the Decanted Demon

December 24th 1896 -  Wee small hours

*A Chronicle of Uncertain Hours: Being an Account of the Hunters of London and Their Endeavours Against the Forces of Darkness*

The grey hours of early morning found our company in a sorry state. Having spent yet another sleepless night in service of a city largely oblivious to their sacrifices, the hunters had gathered at the Camden residence of one Clarissa — a nurse of orphans, and former object of Mister Kyle Nugget's considerable affections — to prepare for what many privately suspected might be their final act upon this mortal stage.

George Banks, the Divine, had been rather inconveniently whisked away to the celestial realms by a brace of heavenly enforcers who had descended upon him without invitation or explanation, seizing him by the arms and departing in the manner of particularly officious supernatural bailiffs. His absence was keenly felt, though the bags of ritual materials he had collected from the Order of the Veiled Dawn on his journey had, mercifully, been left behind. One could not say the same for his serene temperament, which the party might have found most useful in the hours ahead.

Samuel Fitzwilliam, that ancient and monstrous Elizabethan scholar, drifted through the proceedings with the particular detachment of a man who has not required sleep in approximately three centuries. His capacity for physical healing — a gift both remarkable and deeply unpleasant in its application — was called upon first by the battered and bleeding Kyle Nugget, who had the presence of mind to retrieve a wooden spoon from the kitchen before submitting himself to the procedure. The spoon did not survive. Samuel's ghostly tendrils, described by those present as "wisps of ectoplasmic presence stitching wounds together from the inside," did their work admirably if agonisingly, and Kyle emerged from the experience with fewer injuries, marginally more internal bleeding than one might desire, and approximately half a wooden spoon embedded in his gum line. "That really hurt," Kyle was heard to observe with the measured understatement of a man who has recently been mauled by a bear and scratched across the face by a shape-shifting orangutan. "Let us never speak of this again."

Gabriel West submitted himself to the same treatment shortly thereafter, lowering himself onto a gardening stool of insufficient height with the stoic professionalism of a man who has witnessed considerably worse. The Foundation man gripped his own hands with white-knuckled determination, veins standing proud upon his temple, as Samuel worked. It must be noted, in fairness to the ghost, that three centuries without physical sensation renders one rather poor at gauging what constitutes an appropriate amount of healing intensity. "I remember skin being very stretchy," Samuel was heard to murmur, apparently by way of explanation.

With wounds tended to after a fashion, the party permitted themselves a few short hours of rest — draped across whatever horizontal surface presented itself — before the business of the day could commence in earnest. The Elizabethan scholar retired to his quill case to regenerate in the manner peculiar to his condition. The rest slept as people do when they know that waking up might prove considerably worse than not doing so.

Gabriel rose first, in the manner of men who have served long enough in difficult circumstances that their bodies refuse the luxury of proper rest. He set about boiling water for a wash station, tending to his damaged flamethrower backpack with improvised repairs using what appeared to be a pair of old trousers, and performing a meticulous inventory of the ritual materials. Upon the kitchen table, he arranged each item with the precision of a particularly conscientious museum curator: the essence of evil in its ornate phylactery, currently buried in a plant pot in the back garden for the comfort of all present; the lindworm ichor; the real Tablet of the Dead; the soon-to-be-falsified Tablet of the Dead; the hand grenade, now returned to its rightful owner; and the dagger, which continued to belong primarily to itself. Each item was furnished with a small index card in Gabriel's careful handwriting, annotated with origin, date, and in several cases, what appeared to be invented scientific nomenclature. He also, reportedly, discovered what can only be described as ritual cloaks in a downstairs wardrobe. Kyle had insisted they were essential for the ritual. Whether this was true or merely a matter of personal aesthetic preference remained a subject of some debate.

It was Kyle who, venturing out to procure breakfast from a nearby bakery, encountered a familiar figure on the opposite side of the road. Sean, arsonist, express rifleman, and holder of a pocket knife, had apparently spent the better part of the past several days wandering the streets of London in search of his companions. "I've been trying to find you for ages," he explained with the slightly haunted expression of a man who has been walking around Camden for rather longer than is advisable. "Do you sleep or something?" Kyle offered him a pastry — definitively not a brownie, the confection not yet having arrived in England from its American origins — and escorted him back to Clarissa's house, pausing only to warn him not to mention the spare bedroom, not to mention Clarissa's brother, and that there may have been some incidents involving bears.

The reunion at the house was, predictably, conducted at gunpoint. Gabriel levelled his shotgun at Sean with the calm efficiency of a man who has learned that familiar faces are not always attached to familiar souls, and Kyle raised his magnum in fraternal solidarity. A brief interrogation followed, during which Gabriel demanded to know whether Sean had committed a particular act of violence at Harrington Hall — he had — and what his dog's name had been. The dog question proved troublesome. Samuel Fitzwilliam, floating with characteristic imperviousness through the barrel of the shotgun, peered into Sean's eyes and confirmed the absence of the telltale black irises of demonic possession. The party lowered their weapons. Sean produced his grenade. Everyone agreed to calm down.

Preparations for the ritual commenced in earnest. Sean distributed salt across the front facing doorways and window frames with methodical care — Samuel noting the placement with considerable personal interest, as salt presented certain logistical complications for an entity of his particular nature. Gabriel, having completed repairs to his flamethrower, assumed what could only be described as a Gran Torino configuration: settled into an armchair, shotgun across his lap, eyes fixed upon the front door. Kyle descended to the cellar to arrange the ritual space, sweeping the floor, hanging lanterns for what he described as "the right atmosphere," and sketching a pentagram on the flagstones in chalk. The pink notebook containing Kyle's ritual instructions — clearly purchased at some point without regard for period-appropriate aesthetics — was placed upon a small stand. The cloaks were distributed.

It was Gabriel's third eye — a capacity recently developed and still imperfectly understood — that provided the first warning. Scanning the exterior of the property with senses both mundane and otherwise, he detected no obvious surveillance, no familiar faces lurking at lampposts, no curtains twitching. And yet something nagged at the edge of his perception. A twinge, indistinct and troubling, that he could not quite attribute to either the ambient pressure of the essence of evil in the back garden or the more general weight of impending apocalypse. He said nothing definitive. He returned inside. He kept his eyes on the door.

Samuel, charged with a quiet patrol of the upper floors, drifted upstairs in the company of no sound whatsoever — and discovered Sean unconscious on the floor.

The subsequent scene unfolded with the particular controlled panic of people who have been in exactly this situation before. Gabriel sprinted upstairs and knelt over Sean with the practiced efficiency of a man applying field medicine. Sean came round to find a knee on his chest and Samuel Fitzwilliam staring directly into his eyes from approximately four inches away. The safe word — pineapple, selected at some earlier point with a prescience that now proved inspired — was exchanged on the count of three. Everyone was human. Or at least, everyone present was human.

Sean, upon recovering himself, reported only fragmentary memory of the preceding hours. He had been possessed since the library. He had not been in control. And the last thing he clearly remembered before losing himself was seeing Clarissa.

The realisation struck the room with the force of a hammer: Clarissa had departed for work some ten minutes prior. Clarissa, who had heard their plans. Clarissa, from whose mouth Samuel had seen wisps of black smoke still faintly curling as he had examined the unconscious Sean.

"We have to leave," said Samuel. And then, in the ensuing three minutes of very spirited disagreement about whether to leave or to stay or to look for Clarissa or to hide the ritual items in the walls to confuse potential intruders: "We have to leave now."

Gabriel grabbed the real Tablet of the Dead — on the very sound principle that one ought not leave the most important artefact in the world sitting unattended on a kitchen table — and bolted out the back door. A market trader at the end of the alleyway, asked if he had seen anyone run past, pointed down the road with the cheerful directness of a man who has been thoroughly wronged by a stranger in a nurse's uniform. Gabriel began to run, pursuing Clarissa who had quite some head start.

The coach, packed with haste and considerable jostling of occult materials, caught Gabriel on the downhill road. Sean, hanging from the door with his arm extended, hauled the breathless Gabriel onto the footplate in a manoeuvre of considerable theatrical elegance, and the whole party careened down the hill in pursuit of their possessed host. Kyle, perched on the driver's bench with a bowl of water in his lap and a certain object of troll-related provenance acting as a compass — the less said about this the better, and yet it worked — directed the driver toward Clarissa's distant figure.

Gabriel leapt from the coach ahead of the others, knotted with a length of rope to the carriage door as a precaution against possession, and proceeded to wave the real Tablet of the Dead above his head in the manner of a man making the most magnificently reckless distraction in recorded history. The daemon within Clarissa turned its attention toward this inexplicable spectacle.

And Samuel, invisible in the commotion, reached through the metaphysical fabric of the situation and pulled.

The demon came free of Clarissa like smoke drawn through a keyhole, howling and twisting in Samuel's incorporeal grip. Kyle stood ready with the whiskey bottle. The cork went in. Kyle held it shut with both hands while the bottle vibrated with considerable menace. Gabriel applied string and wax from the coach's ample supply of such things while the bottle attempted to communicate its displeasure through the medium of sustained trembling.

Clarissa, restored to herself, stared at Kyle. "Every time I see you," she said, with the weariness of a woman who has catalogued too many impossible things, "something bad happens." She then fainted.

Sean caught her. Gabriel, still tethered to the carriage, could not.

The demon sat in its bottle. The ritual materials sat in their bags. The party sat in Gabriel's coach on a hill in Camden, the whole of London spread before them, and those dark and terrible clouds continuing their slow advance from the southeast like something out of a rather pointed allegorical illustration.

"Off to a train yard, then," said Gabriel. And so they went.

Thus concludes this portion of the account. The demon remains bottled. George remains absent. The world remains, for the moment, extant. Whether it shall remain so is, as ever, a matter of some uncertainty.

# Questions for the Next Session:
* Now that Gabriel has charged into a public street waving the most dangerous artefact in recorded history above his head while tied to a carriage, one must ask: how many witnesses have filed formal reports with the Metropolitan Police, and will the party's disguise of "slightly different clothes" prove sufficient?
* The demon currently resides in what appears to be a whiskey bottle sealed with a cork and string, inside a coach containing several other volatile ritual items — will Kyle be able to resist the temptation to open it, just briefly, to see if it has settled down?
* George Banks is currently receiving what his celestial superiors have described as a "debrief" — is this the kind of debrief that ends with him returning refreshed and healed, or the kind that ends with him being assigned to a different continent entirely?
* The ritual requires two to three days of continuous effort, a demon in a bottle, a troll-related navigational instrument, and the willing cooperation of a group of people who cannot agree on what to call a spare bedroom — what are the realistic odds that all of these things will still be in the same place at the same time?
* Samuel Fitzwilliam has now performed emergency supernatural surgery on two people, learned that larger demons are harder to detect, and had salt scattered liberally throughout what amounts to his home — at what point does he simply refuse to leave the quill case?
