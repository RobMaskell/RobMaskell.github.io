# The Last Stand of The Order of the Veiled Dawn - Part Two

December 25th 1896  

*Being a True and Complete Account of the Concluding Actions of Those Brave and Peculiar Souls Who Saved the World from Ruination*

The scene which confronted our peculiar band of hunters at the commencement of this final chapter was, by any reasonable measure, most desperate. The villain of the piece, the formidable and thoroughly disagreeable Octavian Frostholm, had swept into the Tower of London like some manner of infernal emperor, having come, as one witness might observe, mob-handed to a quite extraordinary degree. His demonic retinue had streamed through the doors in numbers that beggared all reasonable description, and by means of some considerable dark sorcery, he had rendered the assembled hunters quite immobile within invisible, serpentine restraints. With characteristic contempt, he had declared that he would not underestimate these particular individuals a second time — and so it was that Gabriel West, George Banks, Kyle Nugget, and the irrepressible Sean found themselves held fast, whilst the ancient and incorporeal Samuel Fitzwilliam watched proceedings from somewhere considerably safer, peering out with ancient eyes from within a convenient suit of armour.

Frostholm, in his imperious manner, fixed his gaze upon Gabriel and enquired, with the casual menace of a man entirely confident of his position, whether anyone else remained in the upper room. Gabriel met the enquiry with the sort of expression that men reserve for the gravely inevitable and replied, with admirable economy of language, that there was, in point of fact, a bloody army waiting above. Whether this constituted bravado, misdirection, or simple candour, the effect was equally negligible upon the villain, who instructed his legion of demonic foot soldiers to sweep upstairs and deal with any remaining opposition.

It was at this juncture that the restraining spell was released, though this offered precious little relief to the hunters, who now found themselves firmly in the grip of Frostholm's creatures — three demons to each captive, with the unfortunate Sean, who had by this point begun to make a thorough nuisance of himself, attracting no fewer than four. The villain then picked up what he believed to be the great prize — the sacred tablet of the dead, the object of so much suffering and endeavour — and handed it to the sinister Harrington with instructions to return it to Harrington Hall for verification. He was, in this moment, entirely deceived. The tablet in question was a forgery of considerable craft, the genuine article having already been delivered unto angelic safekeeping. The entire edifice of Frostholm's triumph rested upon a magnificent lie.

Sean, whose relationship with restraint was, at the very best, theoretical, managed to liberate one arm from his captors with sufficient violence to draw his silver pocket knife across the abdomen of one unfortunate demon — an act of such precise and terrible sharpness that the creature was compelled to attend to the immediate business of retaining his own intestines. Sean was subsequently restrained by four demons, his knife confiscated, and his hand smashed against the wall for good measure. He bore this with remarkable equanimity, remarking only that he had had his fun, and that he supposed he ought to put the knife away.

The attempt to trip Harrington as that worthy departed with the false tablet was partially successful — the man stumbled, though he did not fall, and regarded Sean with the precise expression of a man who has entirely exhausted his patience. The punishment administered by Harrington's fist, first to Sean's midsection and then, with considerably more enthusiasm, directly to his face, resulted in a nose broken most thoroughly and a countenance somewhat reminiscent of a man who has recently lost an argument with a piston engine. Sean spat blood at his captors with what one can only describe as panache.

Frostholm, having surveyed the room, found nothing to concern him — the hunters had wisely left behind no evidence of their true purpose — and turned to his assembled minions with the air of a man who has entirely finished with an inconvenience. "Just deal with these people," said he, with magnificent dismissiveness. "I never want to see them again. Kill them, tidy up, and return to Harrington Hall." And with that, the great villain swept from the room, leaving his creatures to debate amongst themselves, entirely within earshot of their intended victims, the precise methodology of murder.

It was Gabriel who seized upon this moment with characteristic professional cunning, turning to the demons with a voice of studied casualness and suggesting that, were they to carry out their necessary business, the adjoining room offered a view of the Thames that was, he assured them, quite spectacular. The creatures, persuaded by this recommendation, began to manhandle Gabriel towards the adjacent door — at which point the bucket of holy water, positioned earlier with quiet foresight by the Gabriel himself, descended upon the heads of three demons with entirely satisfying results. The creatures hissed and recoiled, quite overcome by the burning nature of sanctified water upon demonic flesh, and Gabriel found himself, for one glorious moment, entirely free.

He called out the alarm and made immediately for the pile of confiscated weapons.

Meanwhile, George Banks — who had, throughout his captivity, conducted himself with the serene dignity of a man who has faced considerably worse — produced, by means that shall tactfully remain unexplained in polite company, a small holy water device of angelic provenance. It shall merely be recorded that the magnificent Hubert, George's snowy owl and celestial companion, had been entrusted with this device and delivered it through the leaded window at considerable personal velocity, rolling upon landing with what dignity he could muster, before launching it towards George's waiting hand. George caught it, uttered a single word of prayer, and released it upon the floor, where it erupted in a spinning fountain of holy water that caught his own three captors full, partially incapacitated those attending to Kyle and Sean, and contributed materially to the general chaos with great enthusiasm.

Samuel Fitzwilliam, watching from within his armoured concealment, seized this moment to exercise the full terrible weight of his poltergeist abilities. Every object in the room — brackets, ornaments, shelving, and general detritus accumulated over centuries within the Tower of London — lifted simultaneously and began to fly with tremendous purpose at the demonic captors. The ghost's ancient power, honed over three hundred years of scholarly existence, manifested itself with extraordinary results, creating a chaos of flying detritus that occupied the demons' remaining hands and attention most thoroughly. Samuel communicated his intentions to Kyle with characteristic brevity, and Kyle, receiving this intelligence, determined that the situation, whilst undeniably a bit of a pickle, offered at least some avenue for action.

Kyle, bringing his considerable physical presence to bear, crushed two of his captors against the wall with the sort of force that tends to produce unpleasant sounds in the ribcage department. He emerged from this at the cost of a dislocated arm and a degree of pain that a lesser man might have found quite discouraging. Kyle, however, is not a lesser man. He is simply a very large one. He made for the stairs.

Sean, working with patient ingenuity at his jacket throughout the chaos, chose the moment of maximum distraction to slip free of his captors — who had been, as he had correctly assessed, gripping him largely by his outer garments — and departed in the direction of the secret passage with the sort of focused energy that characterises a man of genuinely practical instincts.

In the stairwell, Kyle found two demons waiting with firearms levelled at a target that, it must be said, offered very little opportunity for missing. Samuel, perceiving the danger from across the room, manifested alongside his quill — which Kyle still carried — projecting an image of such profound supernatural horror around the large man's person that the demons hesitated, fired, and found their bullets turned aside by Samuel's magic, deflected at an angle that — as the ghost noted privately — required rather more effort than it might have done for a man of more conventional dimensions. Kyle, thus preserved, shoulder-charged the demons down the staircase with sufficient momentum to deposit them at the bottom in a condition that suggested considerably reduced enthusiasm for further hostilities.

In the upper room, George Banks revealed the full glory of his true nature — six great wings erupting from his frame, ancient eyes regarding the demons from within those wings, the burning sword brought to hand as Gabriel tossed it to him. George, released from the pretence of mortality, moved through the remaining demons with angelic ferocity, blinking about the room in a manner that defied ordinary locomotion. Gabriel, having retrieved his shotgun and loaded it with appropriate ammunition for demonic application, proceeded with methodical and devastating efficiency — legs first, then body shots, working through the creatures with the cold professionalism of a man who has done this rather a lot and intends to survive to do it again. At one point, the timing of Gabriel's shotgun and George's aerial assault coincided imperfectly, resulting in one wing receiving a partial blast. George took this with the equanimity of a being who has endured considerably more, and Gabriel offered the dry observation that it would do well to stay out of his way. Sean, having briefly considered flight, returned and brought his express rifle to bear with considerable satisfaction, having spent a meditative interval adjusting the sights to his precise requirements.

When the last demon had been subdued — broken, burned, shot, or fallen down a considerable flight of stairs — the hunters stood together in a room that resembled nothing so much as a particularly ambitious abattoir, and regarded one another in silence.

"Well," observed Sean, with the quiet understatement of a man who has recently had his nose rearranged. "That was a bit fucked, wasn't it?"

Gabriel, looking around at his bloodied companions, stated simply that he would remain behind to see them out and that they should take the stairs. George, with the grace of a man who recognises a noble lie when he hears one, replied only that it was very noble, and that he'd been informed by extremely high authority that it was only a sin if one witnessed it. Kyle, his arm restored by George's healing touch, expressed his gratitude to Samuel for the preservation of his life with the directness of a man who knows he was, without question, seconds from death.

Hubert, injured by his passage through the window, was carried out in George's arms, complaining at considerable length about the angle and manner of his transport, and receiving in return the most sustained and genuine praise his guardian had ever bestowed upon him.

They walked out of the Tower of London and waited.

In the basement of Harrington House, by candlelight and censer smoke, Frostholm and his retinue performed their great ritual over the blood of an alpha vampire and a magnificent diamond, chanting over what they believed to be the instrument of their world-ending apotheosis. Hours passed. The tablet's hieroglyphs lit in cold blue flame as power flooded through it. And then — as any student of the sublime would have entirely predicted — it shattered. Exploded outward with every fragment of holy power embedded within it by the hunters' earlier sacrifices, the destruction of that false tablet tore through every demonic presence in the chamber. Harrington. Frostholm. Every servant of darkness assembled to witness the end of the world. All of them undone in an instant.
The hunters heard only a dull thud, as of something vast collapsing underground. Harrington Hall folded quietly into the earth.
And then, to the south-east, the storm clouds over Kent — which had gathered for so long and so ominously above the horizon — began, at last, to part. A single beam of winter sunlight descended through the grey, illuminating a stretch of London's rooftops in gold. It was, all agreed, a thoroughly cinematic moment.

The world, improbably, had been saved.

In the years that followed: Gabriel West turned his considerable energies toward the rebuilding of The Foundation, eventually assuming command of its London office and extending to his former colleagues honorary membership in recognition of services that could never properly be recorded. Samuel Fitzwilliam, emboldened by months of experience that three centuries of library-dwelling had entirely failed to provide, turned his considerable intellect toward the mystery of his own death — what truly happened, and why — with occasional light haunting on the side. George Banks, Daveithai to those who knew his true name, was recalled to his proper station between realms by three of his angelic colleagues and returned to his ancient work of watching over souls — though he spends, it is said, a considerable portion of eternity glancing back toward that small, peculiar island. Kyle Nugget retired to a domestic arrangement of remarkable complexity, having resolved the philosophical problem of fidelity by the simple expedient of marrying everyone involved, and remembers most of their's and his children's names. And Sean — good, practical, eminently sensible Sean — returned to South Africa, took up a rocking chair, and many years later, opened a newspaper to read of a most baffling discovery: that the Crown Jewels had, at some undetermined point, gone entirely missing from the Tower of London.

He smiled, and said nothing.

Thus endeth the archive of the twilight of the Veiled Dawn

* Will Hubert the owl ever forgive George for carrying him like that, or shall this grievance be nursed across the eternal span of heaven?
* Kyle's domestic arrangements appear stable, but is anyone keeping track of how many children are actually in that house, and does he know all their names, or just most of them?
