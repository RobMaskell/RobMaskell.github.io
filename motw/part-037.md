# An account of the Investigators' Endeavours at the Slippery Eel

December 23th 1896 - Midday

*Being a True and Faithful Record of the Events of the Fourteenth Session*

The morning found the company ensconced within Gabriel West's magnificent carriage, that most singular conveyance which served as both transport and temporary residence for this most peculiar brotherhood of hunters. Having spent the night in Hackney recovering from the tribulations of their previous endeavour — the acquisition of Lindworm blood, no less — the party stirred themselves to address the considerable matter of redistributing their artefacts and equipment amongst themselves.

The question of who should bear Samuel Fitzwilliam's quill case caused no small amount of deliberation. The ghostly Elizabethan scholar, ever cautious regarding his most prized possession, would not hear of it going to George Banks — the celestial being's tendency toward teleportation rendering him an unreliable custodian. Kyle Nugget, however, had previously demonstrated sufficient reliability, and so into Kyle's capacious pocket the case went, to rest alongside what would soon prove to be an item of terrifying significance: the Raven Shade Dagger, a weapon that only Kyle could touch without mortal consequence.

Gabriel assumed stewardship of the two precious pints of Lindworm blood — though a Foundation man to his core, he could not resist quietly setting aside a modest sample for his employers. One suspects the Foundation's analysts would find the remainder perfectly adequate. George, as befitting a divine being of considerable spiritual authority, took possession of the counterfeit tablet. The Holy Hand Grenade — described by the Keeper himself as possessing "the power to level continents" — was entrusted to Kyle, who placed it in the same pocket as the quill case, a decision that caused visible discomfort among his companions.

With the administration of the company's supernatural inventory resolved, attention turned to the more immediate matter at hand. The party's intelligence suggested that one Jessica Sloan, a woman of professional acquaintance known to Kyle Nugget, might provide some means of locating the elusive Atticus Rook — a former member of the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn and a man of potential use to the company's grand undertaking.

"Are you on good terms?" enquired George, with the particular delicacy of a man who has witnessed rather too much of the human condition.

"I think so," replied Kyle with characteristic optimism. "I can't think of anyone I'm not on good terms with."

George, to his considerable credit, identified this as a matter of some concern.

The company arrived at Jessica's Hackney residence — a modest affair in a rather less than salubrious part of the borough, notable for its unpainted door and knee-high weeds. Three strong knocks from Kyle's substantial fist brought not Jessica, but her housemate to the door. The woman's expression upon potentially recognising Kyle shifted rapidly through several unflattering emotions. Kyle, with admirable composure, introduced himself as "Christian Nugget," a name not previously recorded in any census, and was informed that Jessica was at work — on what the housemate described as the day shift.

The housemate's expression as Kyle departed suggested she was committing his features to memory with some urgency.

With Jessica unavailable at home, the company determined that Samuel might employ his considerable spectral talents to investigate her room for any intelligence regarding Atticus Rook. The ghost of the Elizabethan scholar drifted through the window of the small cottage — and made his way undetected to Jessica's private chamber.

What Samuel found spoke volumes about the woman within. The room was neat and tidy, "much more girlish" than one might expect, as Samuel later relayed — a private sanctuary far removed from the nature of her profession. Two books sat upon the bedside table. Samuel using his magic caused both to float before him, their pages turning in the manner peculiar only to poltergeists of considerable ability.

The first was a diary — a record of private hopes and regrets, including an apparent longing to escape her current circumstances. The second proved considerably more useful: a professional ledger of clients. Under the entry for Atticus Rook, Jessica had noted "well dressed", "nervous", and — most entertainingly — "Why does this man stink of rum?"

Of greater import still was this: Atticus was expected that very afternoon.

Samuel replaced the books with care and made to depart. At the foot of the stairs, he encountered Jessica's housemate, who had returned. Samuel, never one to miss an opportunity for theatre, manifested in full spectral splendour. The woman fled screaming toward the front of the house, her heart rate, one suspects, remaining elevated for the remainder of the day.

"We've killed somebody else," observed George, not entirely incorrectly.

The company regrouped and made haste toward Soho, guided by Kyle's prior knowledge of Jessica's professional establishment — a house of ill repute rejoicing under the name of "The Slippery Eel". The carriage rolled through the streets of Victorian London: flower sellers, market stalls, horse dung, and rather more displayed ankle than George's sensibilities entirely welcomed. The divine being devoted the journey to his Fortunes — his celestial eyes flickering gold as he held three boons from Heaven itself against whatever trials lay ahead.

The Slippery Eel proved to be precisely what one might expect: dusty frontage, tatty curtains, the unmistakable atmosphere of an establishment where business is conducted with rather more candour than a bank.

The party entered in stages. Kyle, his bowler hat lowered judiciously over his eyes, led the way. Gabriel followed, maintaining the expression of a man who is absolutely not here for the services advertised. George proceeded directly to the bar with the purposefulness of a man seeking Dutch courage and enquired after bourbon.

The madam, one Molly Malone — a woman of considerable business acumen — recognised Kyle immediately, though her recognition carried complex emotional undertones. The party settled in to wait, Gabriel finding himself burdened with female company he had not solicited, and George learning from the barman that a rum-scented gentleman had indeed visited the establishment somewhat earlier that very afternoon.

It was George who, through careful conversation and the provision of whiskey, extracted this intelligence, quietly establishing that Atticus Rook's scent — rum and tobacco — matched the barman's recent customer.

When Atticus Rook finally appeared at the door to the main room, one look at three unknown gentlemen — and one particularly suspicious Kyle Nugget — was sufficient to send him retreating back the way he had come. Kyle identified him instantly.

"That's Atticus," he said.

What followed was a pursuit through the somewhat intimate geography of the Slippery Eel. Gabriel kicked down one door, disturbing a client in flagrante delicto — the shotgun he produced inspiring the man toward philosophical discretion rather than indignation. Kyle opened another to find a warm but unoccupied bed. George, meanwhile, rounded the corner of the back hallway with a flamboyance that would not have been out of place at the theatre, and collided directly with Atticus Rook.

The man smelled precisely as Jessica's ledger had suggested.

"If you are who I think you are, I wouldn't mind a quick conversation, dear boy," said George, gripping Atticus as he attempted to depart. "No harm, no foul."

Atticus, a man of nervous disposition and a gammy left leg, was guided — with the assistance of a shotgun across Gabriel's knees and the sudden materialisation of an Elizabethan ghost from Kyle's pocket — into an empty room. The smell of the room was considerable. Gabriel opened the window.

The conversation that followed was illuminating. Atticus Rook, it transpired, harboured a grievance against Kyle Nugget of some personal magnitude. He had intended, once upon a time, to rescue Jessica Sloan from her current occupation. Kyle's interference — the precise nature of which was left diplomatically unspecified — had apparently rendered this aspiration impossible.

"You ruined my relationship with Jessica Sloan," said Atticus, and produced a leather glove with which he struck Kyle smartly across the face, issuing a formal challenge to a duel.

"Is this a recent thing?" asked Kyle, with the tone of a man reviewing a very long list of possible offences.

The challenge was firmly declined for the immediate future, and Gabriel outlined their proposal with admirable clarity: they required information about the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn — specifically, they needed to make contact with its leadership — in exchange for which they would offer Atticus Rook protection. Gabriel revealed his Foundation credentials with deliberate calculation, watching Atticus's composure crumble entirely.

"I'm dead, aren't I?" said Atticus.

"We're all going to be dead if you don't help us," replied Gabriel.

When Samuel was finally introduced — emerging from Kyle's pocket in a swirl of grey mist — Atticus managed to style out his reaction with rather less success than he perhaps intended. The dagger was produced. Atticus recoiled. The mechanics of their situation were made plain: a great evil threatened everything, and Atticus Rook was their means of approaching those who might provide the final ingredient for the company's great working.

Jessica was fetched from upstairs by George, arriving with the purposeful energy of a woman with outstanding financial grievances. Kyle received a Miss Piggy slap of considerable force.

"I left an IOU," he noted.

A bag of tuppences resolved the immediate fiscal matter. Between Atticus's simmering resentment, Jessica's pragmatism, and the prospect of transportation to New York, a deal was struck. George — wings deployed with divine precision, bolstered by Gabriel's instinctive channelling of whatever dark mirror of angelic power he had been developing — transported the pair of them to a quiet corner of Manhattan, depositing them safely in what had once been quiet woodland and was now, rather inconveniently, someone's backyard.

Atticus, gazing at streets that were unmistakably not London, relayed his payment:

"The Grand Lodge is at Thirty-Five Gracechurch Street, in the City."

And then, as an afterthought: "If you can find a way for Kyle to die, that would be fantastic."

"We're working on it," replied George, not entirely untruthfully.

The company reassembled, extracted themselves from the Slippery Eel through the window rather than face Molly's questions, and made their way to Gracechurch Street. The building at number thirty-five was handsome, well-kept, and entirely dark. In the three hours during which the others attended to matters of personal grooming — new clothes, baths, shaves, and the quiet restoration of professional dignity — Samuel observed from the stationary carriage and saw not a single light, not a single visitor.

When George returned, freshly presented and considerably more fragrant, he paused to study the building — and noticed only a vagrant on the steps outside, engaged in the time-honoured art of soliciting charitable contributions from passers-by. George, naturally, was comprehensively fleeced.

The session closed with the company cleaned, presentable, and in possession of an address. Thirty-Five Gracechurch Street awaited them — dark, silent, and entirely unknown in its intentions. What price the essence of evil?

The vagrant counted his coins, well satisfied with the evening's work.

## Questions for the Next Session

* Will Kyle's debt to the Slippery Eel's laundry bill ever truly be settled, or will the tuppence situation haunt him across multiple continents?
* Having now teleported two people to New York, will George attempt to expand his celestial transportation service, and how long before someone ends up in the middle of the Atlantic regardless?
* The vagrant outside Gracechurch Street shook down George with remarkable efficiency — is he simply a very good beggar, or has the Golden Dawn's surveillance operation been running directly under everyone's noses this entire time?
* The Grand Lodge has shown no lights and no visitors for three hours — is there anyone actually inside, and if the party do manage to get through the door, what precisely are the odds of someone immediately trying to enslave Samuel?
* Gabriel has now revealed Foundation credentials to a nervous former occultist who has been shipped to New York with a note and some dollars — how long before the Foundation sends a very politely worded and extremely threatening letter about operational security?
