# The Last Stand of The Order of the Veiled Dawn - Part One

December 25th 1896 - Late Morning  

*Being a Full and Faithful Account of the Affairs of Those Who Stand Between the World and Its Ruin*

The hour was grave, and the fellowship was divided. George Banks, the Divine sent from on high, had found himself summoned once more to account for his mortal transgressions before no less a figure than the Archangel Gabriel himself — the true article, not the rather more weathered Foundation operative who shared the name. The celestial tribunal had been convened in what can only be described as an eccentric chamber of heaven, furnished with the accumulated curiosities of countless ages and civilisations, smelling to each visitor of whatever comfort they held most dear. To Gabriel West, that smell was unmistakably Tennessee bourbon.

The Archangel received George with the weary disposition of a bureaucrat who has filed the same complaint too many times. His charge was plain: the angel had gone native, corrupted by prolonged association with a company of mortals whose moral flexibility was, at best, heroic pragmatism and, at worst, an affront to the very clear and considerably Old Testament code of heaven. George, however, was not without eloquence. He spoke of grey areas, of the peculiar depth of the human race, and of the sheer scale of the catastrophe he had been sent to prevent. "You split it like it's a simple fact," he said, with no small measure of conviction, "but that simply isn't true." He requested, as his advocate, Gabriel West — the mortal one, the man with the flamethrower.

The hench angel dispatched to retrieve Gabriel West appeared in the carriage with a pop that gave Sean, a man of considerable combat instincts, sufficient cause to draw his blade. He was lifted from his seat by the throat for his trouble, deposited back with spine-jarring force, and told, in no uncertain terms, that this particular celestial messenger was not in attendance to be trifled with. Gabriel West agreed to attend the tribunal, bade a measured farewell to his companions, and was whisked away to heaven, arriving in the strange chamber smelling, as he observed with some pleasure, of bourbon.

His testimony before the Archangel was characteristically direct. He acknowledged his own moral flexibility without shame, invoked the calculus of the greater good, and argued that George's judgements, however swift, had always been made in service of the whole of humanity. "I think if you are serious about saving everyone down there, then George's help is required," he said, rather bluntly. The Archangel weighed these words with the gravity appropriate to his station. The verdict came: George might return to earth, in his present form, for the duration of the crisis — after which he was to present himself in heaven without delay. Additionally, as a gesture of considerable goodwill, Gabriel West departed with a bottle of celestial bourbon that had materialised at his feet. He told no one. The Archangel did not appear to expect him to.
George, smiting privileges restored, returned Gabriel to the carriage. Hubert, the magnificently put-upon snowy owl, was already present, having arrived via the roof in the manner of a creature resigned to his duties if not exactly enthusiastic about them. "I was eating a perfectly nice mouse," he observed, "and I've been sent here to help you." George reminded him, with characteristic warmth, that without their success there would be no more mice to eat. Hubert conceded the point.

The carriage trundled to a set of rarely-used railway sidings, use of which had been procured by Sean through his railway contacts, whose stated reason for requiring a disused goods carriage — a fishing trip to Scotland — was received without great scrutiny. The carriage was cleared of accumulated railway paraphernalia, rats, and the assorted detritus of storage. Samuel Fitzwilliam, ancient ghost and developing poltergeist, applied himself to the rats with considerable enthusiasm, his very presence causing them to vacate the premises at speed. Gabriel West discovered a crate of rather fine china — gilded, floral, entirely incongruous — which was repurposed as a dining set. George found a sack of undelivered letters and read one with quiet thoughtfulness, returning it with something approaching admiration for the complexity of mortal feeling. Kyle Nugget emerged wearing a railway conductor's hat and was, by general consensus, wearing it well. Sean located a crate of theatrical props, including revolvers and blank cartridges, and subjected Gabriel West to what can only be described as an impromptu Wild West performance.

Then the real work began.

Kyle, the overweight and formidably capable Expert, directed the casting of the ritual with the authority of a man who had spent an entire sleepless night designing it. Gabriel West audited the process from a clipboard, ticking off each component with the meticulous attention of a man determined that nothing should go wrong. Red wine, appropriated from the cellars of the Amber Society, was consumed in quantity — Kyle cast better when well-lubricated, and he had said as much. The Ravenshade Dagger was handled only by Kyle, as the only member of the party capable of surviving contact with it. The holy hand grenade, the essence of evil in its phylactery, and the false tablet were brought into the circle. A large stone bath was filled with Lindworm Ichor and placed at the centre. Hours passed. Candles were lit with Sean's particular enthusiasm. Herbs were burned. The chanting was, by all accounts, extensive.
By the following morning, the ritual was complete. The false tablet had absorbed the contents of the stone bath, transformed into a devastating facsimile of the true Tablet of the Dead — indistinguishable from the original to all but Kyle himself. Two tablets now existed. One was a bomb. One was not. Only one man present could tell them apart, and he had knocked back his celebratory bourbon in a single motion before anyone else had raised their cup.

The matter of the two captured demons was addressed with characteristic thoroughness. The second demon — the one that had been told, in a moment of regrettable candour, the entire plan — was to be dealt with separately. The first, the ordinary demon, would serve as their unwitting messenger. Hubert was dispatched to heaven, and an angelic agent arrived with a carriage and sufficient discretion to spirit away both the arch-demon in its bottle and the genuine Tablet of the Dead to parts unknown. The request was granted. Heaven, to its credit, also cleaned Gabriel's carriage of every bad thing that had been deposited there over the last few days. Gabriel West expressed his gratitude. The angelic agent expressed his disdain. The carriage was, nevertheless, spotless.

George tended to by Gabriel West's medical kit — a handsome doctor's case, the hacksaw still conspicuously unused — was stabilised, if not yet restored. Samuel Fitzwilliam, in one of his more unsettling demonstrations of ghostly ability, attended to the remainder of George's wounds with spectral efficiency, the process accompanied by a high, otherworldly shriek and leaving behind a gnarled golden scar across George's chest, a permanent testament to the arrow that had nearly felled him.

The plan was, in outline, this: release the first demon from its bottle; allow it to observe the false tablet; permit it to flee to Octavian Frostholm with news of the party's location; and be at the Tower of London before it could return with reinforcements. The lure: convince the demons that the crown jewels were required to destroy the tablet, thereby drawing them to a location of the hunters' choosing. 

The carriage was whipped into reckless motion across the parks of London, Sean having instructed the driver to take the direct path regardless of obstacles. Kyle, anchoring the carriage by virtue of his considerable mass, remained entirely unmoved by the jolting. Everyone else clung to him.

The execution of the demon's escape was somewhat more chaotic than intended — Kyle's famously greasy fingers contributed to an unscheduled fumble, the bottle bouncing twice across the carriage floor before disintegrating, releasing a black smoke that took one appraising look at the assembled hunters and departed at speed. Gabriel West fired several shots from the window to sell the alarm, and Kyle leaned out the opposite window to declare, with admirable theatrical timing, "Oh no. This is the one thing we didn't want to happen."

At the gates of the Tower of London, Sean talked his way past the Beefeaters with the confident bluster of a man who had never quite thought things through but had never quite come unstuck either. Kyle's appearance — chimney sweep trousers, railway conductor's hat, a general air of magnificent disreputability — gave the guards momentary pause, which was resolved by Gabriel West being identified, loudly and inventively, as an American spy being taken into custody. Gabriel played his part with enthusiasm. The gates opened.

Inside, preparations were methodical. Gabriel West located a corridor between the entrance and their chosen room, placed a canister of holy fuel at careful distance from the door, took a chair, and sat down in front of it with the pilot light of his flamethrower lit and the patient disposition of a man who had made his peace with what was coming. George blessed the moat, and erected magic demon barrier between Gabriel and whatever was about to come through the door . Holy water was decanted into vials for throwing. Sean discovered a secret passage and mentioned it to the group with the easy confidence of a man who had always suspected it would be there. The observation was noted that Kyle would absolutely not fit down the secret passage. Kyle did not dispute this.

They did not have long to wait. The sounds of fighting began outside — steel, screaming, and the indignant squawking of ravens. Hubert was dispatched through the window to deal with a pair of demon-possessed ravens, launched with all the ceremony of a carrier-based aircraft and all the enthusiasm of a creature who had been doing this sort of thing for entirely too long.

When the demons came through the door, Gabriel West rose from his chair, fixed them with a look of complete conviction, and announced, "The power of Christ compels you." He then aimed directly at the canister and fired.

The explosion was, by all accounts, thorough. The corridor became a killing ground. Demons who could not advance met demons who could not retreat, and the holy conflagration between them was brief, decisive, and extremely loud. Gabriel West emerged with a singed beard.

Samuel Fitzwilliam, in full and terrifying librarian mode, threw himself at Harrington Frostbane how appeared through the door at the rear of his mob, with spectral fury, hair writhing, skull-face alight with something that had no name in the mortal tongue. Patricia — Kyle's former wife, her vessel now crumbling — fought back with equal ferocity, flinging Samuel through a wall with a blast of dark magic. Sean's revolvers spoke twice, iron shot clipping Patricia's vessel. Kyle's magnum roared, the dagger in his other hand ready. George arrived with burning sword and the righteousness of a being who had been very pointedly unable to smite anyone for several days, and delivered a blow of such force that Patricia's vessel surrendered the ghost, and the demon within fled smoking back through the door.

And then Octavian Frostholm stepped through.

He was already casting as he entered. In a moment, all four of the hunters found themselves fixed in place — arms pinned, bodies bound, weapons held but useless. Gabriel West struggled against the constriction with a surge of barely-controlled magic that drew blood from the effort, the spell tightening in response. A thin line of red traced his lip. "Looks like not as strong as you might have thought," he managed.

Octavian regarded them. "I underestimated you lot," he said. "A mistake I'm not going to make again. Now — where is my tablet of the dead?"

The tablet was in the room behind them. The demon was standing between them and every exit. And the false Tablet of the Dead, indistinguishable from the real one, was fifteen feet away.

The chronicle pauses here, as all good chronicles must, at the moment of greatest peril. What follows shall be recorded in due course.

# Questions for the Next Session:
* Will Gabriel West's singed beard improve or worsen his overall appearance, and will he attempt to claim it was intentional?
* Given that Kyle cannot fit down the secret passage, will he be left behind as a very large and extremely irritated distraction, or will George attempt to actively and purposefully defenestrate him?
* Will Octavian Frostholm actually be fooled by the fake tablet, or will he immediately identify the one thing Kyle spent an entire sleepless night creating?
* Now that heaven has the real Tablet of the Dead, is there any possibility that an angel accidentally opens it during light filing duties?
* With the celestial bourbon depleted, will Gabriel West attempt to loot the Tower of London's wine cellar before, during, or immediately after the final confrontation?
